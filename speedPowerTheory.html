Here is where things get interesting. On a still day, w = 0, and if the speed of the bike doubles, then FWIND increases  fourfold ! Therefore, most significant gains can be made in the reduction of forces slowing a bike down by reducing this force. But how ? In reality, there are only two practical ways to reduce FWIND.
1. Reduce CD, the drag coefficient of the bike and rider. This has the effect of creating laminar airflow past the cyclist rather than  turbulent airflow, which would tend to 'suck' a rider backwards to a degree. Most cyclists do this unknowlingly by wearing tight clothing, using aero spokes, using disk wheels, using aero helmets and similar strategies. Going into the drops or an 'aero tuck' position generally also achieves a reduction in CD.
2. Reduce A, the frontal area presented by the cyclist to the wind. Again, most cyclists do this naturally by going into the drops, or into an aero tuck position.  Interestingly, the correlation between body mass and frontal area is not in direct proportion, so a larger rider generally would have more power for a proportionatly smaller increase in frontal area. This explains why bigger riders are often faster on the flats.
